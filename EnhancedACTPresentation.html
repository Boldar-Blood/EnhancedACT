Big Picture: The Enhanced ACT (2025+)

  • Core structure
    • Three required sections: English, Math, Reading (EMR).
    • Optional sections: Science (now optional like Writing) and Writing.
  • Scoring basics
    • Composite ACT score now = average of EMR only (1–36).
    • Science is scored separately (1–36) and used with Math to form the STEM score.
    • ACT Superscore uses EMR only; Science does not affect Superscore.
  • Rollout timeline
    • Digital/online Enhanced ACT: April 2025 onward.
    • Paper ACT transitions by late 2025; legacy paper format retires.
  • Implication for us
    • Students now have to decide whether to add Science.
    • Colleges & scholarships can choose whether and how to use the Science score.

This slide gives the high-level “what changed” beyond just Science:

  • The Enhanced ACT shortens the test and simplifies the composite by focusing only on English, Math, and Reading.
  • Science becomes optional but lives alongside Math in the STEM score.
  • Superscoring has changed: ACT’s official Superscore ignores Science. This matters for how colleges see “best scores.”

Stress that: - From a student/family perspective, ACT is no longer a single fixed package; it’s more customizable. - From a counseling perspective, that customization introduces new strategic decisions and new policy noise.

You don’t need to recite timing minutiae; just make clear that: - Spring 2025 = start of the new era. - By the time current sophomores/juniors are testing, Enhanced ACT will be standard.

What Changed in ACT Science?

  • From required to optional
    • Previously: Science was required and part of the composite.
    • Now: Science is optional, scored separately, and not in the composite or ACT Superscore.
  • Format & role
    • Still a 40-question, data-analysis-heavy section (1–36 scale).
    • Pairs with Math to produce a STEM score that colleges may still value.
  • Reporting
    • Students can register with or without Science.
    • Score reports show:
      • EMR scores
      • Composite (EMR only)
      • Science score (if taken)
      • STEM score (if Science is taken)
  • Key takeaway
    • Science is no longer “baked in” to the composite — but it still exists as a signal, especially for STEM-leaning students and some scholarships.

Here, emphasize the “feel” of Science relative to students’ experience:

  • The content and skills of ACT Science are largely unchanged: interpreting charts, graphs, and experimental setups, not long biology lectures.
  • The big shift is policy and math, not the nature of questions.

Counseling implications: - A weak Science score no longer drags down the composite or the ACT Superscore. - A strong Science score can create a nice STEM story: strong Math + Science, good for engineering, pre-med, etc. - Students may assume “optional” = “doesn’t matter.” Our job is to explain that optional means colleges have choices, not that Science is irrelevant.

If asked, you can briefly note that there is a small extra fee for adding Science, but the main concern is time and fatigue on test day.

Why Optional Science Still Matters

  • STEM readiness signal
    • ACT’s STEM benchmark uses Math + Science to predict success in first-year STEM courses and persistence in STEM majors.
    • Higher Math/Science performance is linked to better STEM outcomes.
  • Predictive value
    • ACT scores (with HSGPA) still add predictive power for college GPA and degree completion.
    • STEM courses often grade more stringently; strong Math/Science scores can help contextualize a transcript.
  • Practical implication
    • Even though Science won’t raise the composite, a solid Science + STEM score can:
      • Strengthen applications for engineering, CS, pre-med, and other STEM majors.
      • Support scholarship or honors-program review that looks at STEM indicators.
  • Rule of thumb
    • If STEM is on the table at all, Science is usually worth taking at least once.

Use this slide to push back gently against the “I hate science so I’ll just skip it” narrative, especially for younger students who might change their minds.

Key ideas: - ACT’s own research shows that Math + Science performance matters for STEM success, and they built a formal STEM benchmark around that. - Colleges and programs that care about STEM will keep looking at both Math and Science if they’re available.

How to phrase it to families: - “Even if Science doesn’t change the composite, it can still tell a helpful story if your student is aiming at anything math- or science-heavy.” - “If there’s any chance of STEM, we want that option open rather than closed.”

You can also point out that a good Science score never hurts; now that it doesn’t drag down the Superscore, it’s mostly upside.

What Colleges & Common App Are Doing

  • ACT’s stance
    • Science is now “student choice”; colleges can require, recommend, ignore, or superscore it as they see fit.
  • Common App
    • Starting with the 2025–26 cycle, students can self-report:
      • An ACT with Science or
      • An ACT without Science
    • This signals broad acceptance of both reporting paths.
  • Elite colleges (current examples)
    • Harvard: “The optional science portion of the new ACT is not required.”
    • Stanford: ACT’s optional Science section is also optional for Stanford.
    • Princeton: Accepts the redesigned ACT; Science is optional for the new test version.
  • Bottom line
    • Many highly selective schools are comfortable with ACT submissions without Science.
    • But programs and scholarships can still have stricter expectations than the general admission office.

This slide reinforces that “optional” is real at many top schools:

  • Harvard, Stanford, Princeton, and others have gone on record that the optional ACT Science section is not required for their applications.
  • Common App supporting ACT with or without Science is a strong signal that schools are prepared for both types of score reports.

However, stress the nuance: - “The college” may not require Science, but specific programs (engineering, nursing, pre-health, selective STEM honors) might still strongly prefer or expect it. - As policies evolve, you’ll maintain a shared tracking document so the team doesn’t have to chase every nuance alone.

Keep it simple in your talk, then let counselors read the notes and references if they want more detail.

Florida Bright Futures: ACT Science Flexibility

  • New rule (starting with 2025–26 graduating class)
    • For Bright Futures, the ACT Science section is now optional.
  • How Bright Futures calculates the ACT composite
    • If ACT scores with Science are reported:
      • Composite = (English + Math + Reading + Science) ÷ 4.
    • If ACT scores without Science are reported:
      • Composite = (English + Math + Reading) ÷ 3.
    • Bright Futures uses the highest section scores across sittings.
  • What this means in practice
    • Students can qualify for scholarships with or without Science, depending on which route yields the higher composite.
    • Florida public universities themselves do not require the Science subsection for admission.
  • Talking point for Florida families
    • “Bright Futures lets you use whichever ACT combination (with or without Science) gives you the stronger composite — we’ll plan your testing to keep both options open.”

Here, explicitly connect to what the COO asked for:

  • Bright Futures now formally allows ACT composites built either with EMRS or just EMR, using the best subscores.
  • That effectively means students can “use or not use” Science, whichever gets them to the needed scholarship cutoff.

How to explain it to families: - “If you take ACT with Science, Bright Futures will average all four subscores.” - “If you take ACT without Science, they’ll average three subscores.” - “They’ll use your best section scores overall. We’ll look at both pathways and see which composite helps you reach the Bright Futures threshold.”

Key counseling takeaway: - For Florida students, taking ACT with Science at least once keeps the scholarship route flexible. - If Science ends up being a weakness, Bright Futures policy gives us a way to ignore it; if it’s a strength, we take advantage of it.

Counseling Framework: Should My Student Take ACT Science?

  1. Clarify likely majors
    • Interested in engineering, CS, data science, health, or “maybe STEM?”
      → Recommend ACT with Science (at least once).
    • Solidly non-STEM (humanities, arts, some business) and clearly non-STEM college list?
      → Science becomes more optional.
  2. Check college & scholarship policies
    • Use a shared spreadsheet with columns:
      • Test required/optional?
      • ACT Science required/recommended/optional?
      • Superscore rules and STEM program notes.
  3. Consider test-day strategy
    • Strong EMR but limited stamina or test anxiety?
      • For non-STEM students with no Science requirements: consider skipping Science.
    • Unclear major or evolving list?
      • Take ACT with Science at least once, then decide about later sittings.
  4. Revisit before senior-year registrations
    • Before final test dates, confirm:
      • College list policies
      • Scholarship requirements (especially Bright Futures and institutional merit)
    • Adjust plan accordingly.

This slide is designed as a scriptable decision tree:

  • Start with major/interest: STEM interest almost always pushes toward including Science.
  • Then move to policies, not vibes: What do the colleges and scholarships actually say on their websites?
  • Next, fold in student stamina and stress. For some non-STEM students, a shorter test may be a real benefit.
  • Finally, remind counselors that a student’s list and interests move over time; what’s clear in 10th grade may be different by 11th.

Encourage counselors to: - Use the spreadsheet as the “single source of truth” so they’re not each reinventing the wheel. - When something on a college website is ambiguous (“may consider STEM components”), send it to you for interpretation and to update the shared notes.
